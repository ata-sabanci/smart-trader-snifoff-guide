Chapter 1

The Dashboard

Your control panel — every piece of market intelligence in one place.

The dashboard is a multi-row panel that appears on your chart. It combines all the indicator's analysis into a single view you can read top-to-bottom. Think of it as your market situation report — updated on every closed candle.

Color Coding System

Colors in the dashboard are not random. Every color has a consistent meaning across the entire indicator:

Green — Bullish / Positive / Supportive
Red — Bearish / Warning / Opposing
Gold — Caution / Mixed / Transitioning
Cyan — Extreme / Shock / Exceptional
Purple — Reversal / Cascade confirmed
Grey — No data / Insufficient / Neutral

Desktop vs Mobile

The Desktop Dashboard shows the full multi-row panel with all active modules. The Mobile Dashboard is a slim 5-row version showing only the most critical data — trend direction, move quality, and volume pressure. Toggle mobile mode in settings when trading on a phone.

Tip: Read the dashboard from top to bottom. Trend direction tells you WHERE the market is going. Efficiency tells you HOW WELL it's going there. Volume tells you WHO is pushing it.
Chapter 2

Trend Engine

Multi-scale direction analysis — know the trend before the crowd does.

The Trend Engine analyzes price direction across 6 different time horizons simultaneously — from 3 bars (fastest) to 49 bars (slowest). It then combines them into a single, actionable signal. Think of it as 6 different analysts watching the same chart at different zoom levels, and the engine reports whether they agree or disagree.

Alignment Score

Each of the 6 scales votes: +1 (bullish), −1 (bearish), or 0 (flat). Add them up and you get the Alignment Score, which ranges from −6 (every scale bearish) to +6 (every scale bullish).

Example: If 5 out of 6 scales say "up" and 1 says "down", the alignment is +4. That's a solid uptrend with near-total agreement.

The 11 Trend States

The engine resolves into one of 11 states, ordered by priority (highest-priority signals override lower ones):

⚡ Bullish Reversal Confirmed ▲
The short-term AND mid-term scales have both turned bullish while the long-term is still bearish. Two independent layers confirm the turn — this is the strongest reversal signal.
Example: Price has been falling for days. Now the 3, 7, 13, and 19-bar scales all point up — only the 29 and 49-bar still point down. The reversal is confirmed from the inside out.
⚡ Bearish Reversal Confirmed ▼
Mirror of above — short and mid-term turned bearish while the broad structure is still bullish. Two layers confirm the turn downward.
Example: A strong uptrend is active, but now 4 of 6 scales flipped bearish. The trend is cracking from the inside.
▲ Bullish Reversal Forming
Only the fastest scales (3 and 7-bar) have turned bullish while the slowest (29 and 49-bar) still point down. This is an early warning — the reversal is building but not yet confirmed by the mid-term.
Example: After a downtrend, the very short-term momentum turns up, but the bigger picture hasn't caught up yet. Watch for the mid-term to follow.
▼ Bearish Reversal Forming
Fastest scales turned bearish while the slowest still point up. Early bearish warning.
▲▲ Strong Uptrend
5 or 6 out of 6 scales agree on bullish direction (alignment ≥ +5). Near-total consensus — the trend is established and clear.
Example: Every time horizon from 3 bars to 49 bars says "up". This is as clear as it gets.
▼▼ Strong Downtrend
5 or 6 out of 6 scales agree on bearish direction (alignment ≤ −5). Near-total bearish consensus.
▲ Hidden Bullish Bias
Short-term is messy and unclear, but the two largest scales (29 and 49-bar) both point up. There's a structural bullish lean that the short-term noise is hiding.
Example: The market looks choppy on small timeframes, but zoom out — the big picture is quietly bullish. Short-term noise masks the real direction.
▼ Hidden Bearish Bias
Short-term is noisy, but the structural scales both point down. The underlying direction is bearish despite surface confusion.
▲ Uptrend Active
3 or 4 scales agree on bullish direction (alignment +3 to +4). A majority says "up" — the trend is active but not at full consensus yet.
▼ Downtrend Active
3 or 4 scales agree on bearish direction (alignment −3 to −4). Majority bearish.
◆ No Clear Trend
Fewer than 3 scales agree on any direction. The market is confused — different time horizons contradict each other. No reliable directional edge exists right now.
Example: The 3-bar says up, 7-bar says down, 13-bar says up, 29-bar says down. Total disagreement — stay cautious or wait.

Conviction

Direction alone isn't enough — you need to know how strong the moves behind it are. Conviction measures the power of the moves across all scales, not just their direction.

HIGH
Powerful moves across multiple time horizons. The direction has strong muscle behind it. Take the signal seriously.
MODERATE
Normal move strength. The direction is there but the energy is average.
LOW
Weak momentum despite a possible directional signal. The moves are timid — wait for confirmation before committing.

Trend Narrative

Below the signal, the dashboard shows a 3-line human-readable summary:

Line 1 — What: The current trend state in one sentence (e.g., "Strong uptrend established.")

Line 2 — Why: The structural detail behind it (e.g., "Near-total agreement across all time horizons.")

Line 3 — How strong: Conviction context (e.g., "Moves are powerful and decisive — high confidence.")

Chapter 3

Candle Efficiency

Move Quality — is the market moving with purpose or spinning its wheels?

Efficiency measures how much of a candle's total energy was converted into a clear directional move. A candle can be very active (large range) but accomplish nothing directional (close near open, huge wicks). Efficiency captures this distinction.

Simple analogy: Imagine a runner. Running 100 meters in a straight line = HIGH efficiency. Running 100 meters but zigzagging and ending up only 10 meters from the start = LOW efficiency. Same energy spent, very different result.

The Efficiency Percentage

Shown as something like "▲ 73.2%". The arrow (▲/▼) shows the direction of the composite move, and the percentage shows how much of the candle group's total motion became directional displacement.

Regime Labels — What Quality Looks Like

Shock! ≥ 100%
An extraordinary event. The candle moved more directionally than its total range suggested was possible. Extremely rare — think flash crash, surprise news, massive breakout.
Example: A central bank announces an emergency rate cut. The candle explodes in one direction with almost no wick. This is a shock event.
Efficient 50% — 100%
Clean, purposeful move. More than half the candle's energy went into directional displacement. This is what a healthy trending market looks like.
Example: A candle with a solid body and small wicks. It opened, moved decisively, and closed near its extreme. Clean trend behavior.
Normal 20% — 50%
Average market behavior. Some direction, some noise. Not remarkable in either direction. This is the default state most of the time.
Example: A regular candle on a regular day. Some body, some wicks. The market moved a bit but nothing dramatic.
Choppy < 20%
No useful directional movement. The candle had range (it moved up and down) but accomplished almost nothing — the close is very near the open. Like someone running in place.
Example: A doji candle with long wicks on both sides. The market was active but buyers and sellers cancelled each other out. No direction. Don't expect continuation from this.

Adaptive Rank (Percentile)

The raw percentage alone doesn't tell you if a move is unusual. An 80% efficiency might be normal for one instrument but extreme for another. The Adaptive Rank solves this by comparing the current efficiency against the last 100 bars automatically:

Extreme Top 5% of recent history
This move is stronger than 95% of recent moves. Something unusual is happening.
Strong Top 25%
Above average — a meaningful move by recent standards.
Normal Middle 50%
Average for this instrument. Nothing remarkable.
Weak Bottom 25%
Weaker than most recent moves. The market is sluggish.

Efficiency Momentum — Where Quality is Heading

Momentum tells you the trajectory of move quality — is it getting better or worse?

Accelerating
Quality is surging. Recent moves are much stronger than the baseline. The trend is gaining power — strong continuation expected.
Sharpening
Quality is gradually improving. A healthy sign — the market is becoming more directional over time.
Steady
No significant change in quality. Stable — no stress, but no new energy either.
Fading
Quality is declining. Moves are getting weaker. Watch for exhaustion — the trend may be running out of fuel.

Trend Coupling — Efficiency Meets Direction

The real power comes from combining efficiency with the active trend. A highly efficient bearish candle during a bullish trend is a warning, not a green light. The indicator produces 19 coupled states:

When efficiency SUPPORTS the trend (Aligned):

⚡ BULL/BEAR SURGE
Extreme efficiency backing the trend. Explosive, high-conviction move.
BULL/BEAR CONFIRMED
Strong efficiency supporting the trend. Solid, reliable continuation.
Bull/Bear Active
Normal efficiency in the trend direction. Standard trend behavior.
Bull/Bear Stalling
Weak efficiency despite matching direction. The trend is losing steam.

When efficiency FIGHTS the trend (Opposed):

⚠ STRONG COUNTER
Extreme efficiency against the trend. Serious danger — this move threatens the trend.
Pressure
Strong push against the trend. A warning shot.
Mild Resistance/Support
Moderate counter-move. Probably a normal pullback within the trend.
Noise
Negligible move against the trend. Ignore it.

When there is NO active trend (Neutral):

Extreme Move Strong Move Normal Indecisive
Without a trend to couple against, the state simply reflects the raw quality level.
Chapter 4

Volume Intelligence

Who's buying, who's selling, and how much pressure is behind it.

Volume tells you the intensity behind price moves. A price increase on heavy volume is more meaningful than one on thin volume. This module decomposes total volume into buying and selling components, then ranks and classifies the result.

Volume Decomposition Methods

📐

Geometry Method

Available on all plans. Uses the candle's shape to estimate the buy/sell split. If the close is near the high, more volume is attributed to buying. If near the low, more to selling. Simple and effective.

🔍

Intrabar Method

Requires Essential+ plan. Uses real lower-timeframe data to classify volume more precisely. Set your preferred lower timeframe (1 minute recommended) in settings.

Key Volume Metrics

Total Volume

The full volume of the candle — all buying and selling combined.

Buy Volume

The estimated portion of volume attributed to buying pressure.

Sell Volume

The estimated portion of volume attributed to selling pressure.

Delta Buy − Sell

The difference between buy and sell volume. Positive delta = buyers dominated that candle. Negative delta = sellers dominated. This is the single most important volume number — it tells you who won.

Volume Zones

How the current volume ranks against recent history, automatically adjusted per instrument:

Explosive Volume
Top 5% of recent history. Something significant is happening — a breakout, news event, or major shift. Pay attention.
Elevated Volume
Above average. Real market interest. Moves backed by this volume tend to be more reliable.
Moderate Volume
Normal market activity. Nothing unusual.
Very Low Volume
The market is quiet. Moves on low volume are less reliable — they can reverse easily.

Chapter 5

Volume Footprint

Not just how much volume — but WHERE it happened at each price level.

Regular volume tells you the total number of shares or contracts traded in a candle. The Volume Footprint goes deeper: it shows you the volume at each price level within a group of candles. This reveals where the real battles between buyers and sellers happened.

Key Concepts

The Point of Control is the single price level where the most total volume (buying + selling combined) traded within a grouping period. Think of it as the "fair value" — the price that the most participants agreed on.

Example: In a 13-candle group, imagine all the volume distributed across every price level. If $142.50 had the most volume, that's the POC. It tells you: "This is where the market found the most agreement."

Why it matters: POC levels act as magnets. When price moves away from a POC, it often returns to it. The more grouping scales that agree on a similar POC price, the stronger that magnet effect.

Max Buy Price: The price level where the heaviest buying volume was concentrated. This is where buyers were most aggressive.

Max Sell Price: The price level where the heaviest selling volume was concentrated. This is where sellers were most aggressive.

Why it matters: If Max Buy is near the lows of the range, buyers are defending a floor (potential support). If Max Sell is near the highs, sellers are capping price (potential resistance).

The price levels with the least buying or selling volume. These are weak zones — price passed through them quickly without much participation.

Why it matters: Weak volume zones offer less resistance. If price revisits these levels, it may pass through easily — they're not defended.

The indicator computes a POC for each of the 6 grouping scales (3, 7, 13, 19, 29, 49 bars). When multiple scales have their POC near the same price, that creates consensus.

"4 of 6 Scales Agree" means 4 different time horizons all found their highest-volume price near the same level. This is a very strong signal that this price is important.

Example: The 7-bar POC is at $85.40, the 13-bar at $85.55, the 19-bar at $85.30, and the 29-bar at $85.45. Four scales cluster around $85.40 — that's a powerful volume center.

Footprint Methods

📐

Geometry (All Plans)

Estimates volume distribution across price levels using the candle's shape. Works everywhere, no special plan needed.

🔬

Native (Premium+)

Uses real tick-level data from TradingView for exact buy/sell volume at each price row. Maximum precision. Requires Premium or Ultimate plan.

Chapter 6

Candle Grouping & Leader Candles

See the market's structure at multiple zoom levels simultaneously.

What Are Grouping Boxes?

The indicator draws transparent boxes showing the price range (highest high to lowest low) of the last 3, 7, 13, 19, 29, and 49 candles. These boxes nest inside each other like Russian dolls — the 3-bar box is the smallest and innermost, the 49-bar box is the largest and outermost.

Why this matters: Each box shows a different time horizon. The 3-bar box is very recent action. The 49-bar box is the broader structural range. When price approaches the edge of a larger box, it's reaching a significant structural boundary.

Leader Candles

A Leader Candle merges all candles in a grouping period into a single composite candle — one OHLC candle that summarizes the entire group. Instead of seeing 7 noisy candles, you see one clear leader that shows the group's open, high, low, and close.

When leader candles are enabled, regular chart candles are hidden, giving you an uncluttered structural view of the market.

Leader Trend Lines

Trend lines connecting the highs and lows of Leader 6 (49-bar group) to Leader 3 (13-bar group), projected forward into the future. These lines show the structural slope of the market — where the market is headed based on the relationship between the broad structure and the mid-term structure.

Lines lock after drawing and refresh on a configurable cycle (default: every 7 bars). Between refreshes, lines stay frozen as reference points.

Live Volume Spike (L1)

🔴 LIVE: Volume Spike

This is the only alert that monitors the current open bar. It fires when the live bar's volume is more than 2× the previous bar AND ranks in the top 5% of the last 49 bars.

Important: Set this alert's frequency to "Once Per Bar" (not "Once Per Bar Close") because it monitors real-time data. Values may fluctuate while the bar is open — once the bar closes, everything locks permanently.

Alert Message Content

Every alert message includes: the symbol name, timeframe, the signal description, the current alignment or efficiency or volume rank value, the price, and the timestamp. You can use these in TradingView's alert notification templates.
